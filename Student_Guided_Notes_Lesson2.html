Why This Matters

Understanding which phase DNA replication happens in is the foundation for everything that follows. When replication goes wrong and errors slip through, the cell's genetic instructions are corrupted. If those errors are not caught at a checkpoint, they get copied into every daughter cell. That accumulation across generations of cells is how a single mutation becomes thousands — and how cancer begins.

Why This Matters

The distinction between gain-of-function and loss-of-function mutations is not just conceptual — it determines the treatment approach. A drug that blocks an overactive RAS protein (stuck accelerator) is mechanistically different from a therapy that compensates for lost p53 function (no brakes). These are different problems that require different solutions. This is exactly why identifying the type of mutation in a patient's cancer matters clinically.

Why This Matters

The two-hit hypothesis explains why inherited cancer predisposition is clinically significant. A person born with one already-mutated copy of a tumor suppressor gene needs only one more mutation to completely lose that checkpoint — a much shorter path to cancer than someone who starts with two functional copies. This is why genetic screening programs at children's cancer hospitals focus on identifying families who carry inherited mutations before cancer develops.

Why This Matters

Simulators let you test what scientists cannot always test ethically in a living person — you can run the same cell under four different genetic conditions and compare outcomes. This is how real computational biologists at research hospitals model cancer: they change one variable, observe the effect, and use those observations to predict how a patient's cells will behave. What you just did is, at its core, how modern precision medicine begins.

Why This Matters

Maya's case represents the biology of the most common childhood cancer. Advances in pediatric cancer research have helped bring the survival rate for ALL from under 10% in the 1960s to over 90% today — and that progress came from understanding the specific cellular and genetic mechanisms driving the disease. Knowing which checkpoint failed and which gene is mutated determines which treatment approach can target it most precisely.
