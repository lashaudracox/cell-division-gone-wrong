Section 4: The Mitotic Phase

Study Notes: The Six Stages of Mitosis

After watching Video 1.3 (Amoeba Sisters — Mitosis) in the main lesson, use these detailed notes to review and deepen your understanding of each stage. Fill in the blanks as you study. The mnemonic PMAT helps you remember the order: Prophase → Metaphase → Anaphase → Telophase.

Before Mitosis: Interphase (review)

The cell spends most of its time in interphase (G1, S, G2). During S phase, DNA is copied, so each chromosome now has two identical sister chromatids joined at the centromere. The cell enters mitosis with everything ready to be divided.

📍 Stage 1: Prophase

What's happening:

  • Chromosomes condense and become visible under a microscope
  • Each chromosome consists of two identical sister chromatids joined at the centromere
  • Centrioles move to opposite poles of the cell (in animal cells)
  • Spindle fibers begin to form from the centrosomes
  • Nuclear envelope begins to break down

Key visible change: Red X-shaped chromosomes become visible in the cell nucleus.

Check your understanding:

What shape do chromosomes appear in during prophase, and why?

📍 Stage 2: Prometaphase

What's happening:

  • Nuclear envelope completely disintegrates
  • Spindle fibers now have direct access to chromosomes
  • Each chromosome has a kinetochore (protein structure) on the centromere
  • Spindle fibers attach to kinetochores on both sides of each chromosome
  • Some spindle fibers don't attach to chromosomes; they form the pole-to-pole framework

Key visible change: Chromosomes start being pulled from both directions; they begin to move.

Note: Some textbooks fold prometaphase into late prophase — they often say there are 4 stages (PMAT) rather than 5.

📍 Stage 3: Metaphase

What's happening:

  • All chromosomes align at the cell's center (the equatorial plate or metaphase plate)
  • This alignment is crucial for accurate distribution to daughter cells
  • Spindle fibers from both poles are still pulling on the chromosomes
  • This is the most recognizable stage — the cell looks like it has a "belt" of chromosomes around its middle

Key visible change: Chromosomes form a perfect line across the cell's center.

🔍 Quality Control Point:

The cell has a checkpoint at metaphase (the spindle assembly checkpoint) to ensure all chromosomes are properly attached to spindle fibers before proceeding. Unattached chromosomes trigger a "wait" signal. You'll learn more about this in Section 6.

📍 Stage 4: Anaphase

What's happening:

  • Sister chromatids separate at the centromere
  • Once separated, each chromatid is now called an individual chromosome
  • Spindle fibers shorten, pulling the separated chromosomes toward opposite poles
  • The cell elongates, getting longer as chromosomes move apart
  • By the end of anaphase, the cell has two complete and identical sets of chromosomes

Key visible change: Chromosomes move to opposite ends of the cell, appearing as V-shapes.

Check your understanding:

Why is it important that sister chromatids are pulled to opposite poles (and not both to the same pole)?

📍 Stage 5: Telophase

What's happening:

  • Chromosomes decondense (relax) and become less visible
  • They form a diffuse mass of chromatin again (like interphase)
  • Nuclear envelopes reform around each set of chromosomes
  • Each new nucleus now has its own complete set of genetic instructions
  • Spindle fibers disappear
  • The cell is now ready for cytokinesis

Key visible change: Two separate nuclei form, one at each pole.

⚠️ Important:

Telophase ≠ End of cell division. Telophase completes mitosis (nuclear division), but the cell is still one cell. Cytokinesis (next section) physically separates it into two daughter cells.

🎯 Summary: The Complete Sequence

Prophase: Chromosomes condense, spindle forms
→ Prometaphase: Nuclear envelope dissolves, spindle attaches
→ Metaphase: Chromosomes align at center
→ Anaphase: Sister chromatids separate, move to poles
→ Telophase: Chromosomes decondense, nuclei reform
→ Cytokinesis: Cell physically divides into two (see next section)

📍 Important term: Apoptosis

When damage is too severe to repair, the cell can undergo apoptosis — programmed cell death. This is the body's way of safely eliminating broken cells before they cause harm.
