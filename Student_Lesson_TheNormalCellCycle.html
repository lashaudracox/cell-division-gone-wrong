Section 1 of 4 · The Normal Cell Cycle
The Four Phases
What the cell does before it divides
What You Will Learn in This Section
›Name the four phases (G1, S, G2, M) and describe what happens in each
›Understand why the cell cycle is a highly regulated sequence, not a random process

Every cell in your body follows a tightly controlled sequence before it divides. This sequence — the cell cycle — ensures that each new cell receives an exact, undamaged copy of the genome. There are about 37 trillion cells in the human body, and this process happens millions of times per second.

Why a Cycle?
The cell cycle is circular — not linear. Once a cell completes division, the two daughter cells can each enter G1 and begin the cycle again. This is how tissues grow, repair, and renew.
PhaseFull NameWhat HappensTime
G1 Gap 1 (Growth) Cell grows larger. Produces proteins and organelles. Checks nutrient levels and receives growth signals. The G1/S checkpoint evaluates whether conditions are right to proceed. ~11 hrs
S Synthesis DNA is replicated — every chromosome is copied exactly once. Each chromosome becomes two identical sister chromatids held together at the centromere. DNA replication errors here can become mutations. ~8 hrs
G2 Gap 2 (Prep) Cell continues growing. Verifies that replication was accurate. Checks for double-strand DNA breaks. Assembles proteins needed for cell division. The G2/M checkpoint is the last quality check before mitosis. ~4 hrs
M Mitosis & Cytokinesis The cell physically divides. The nucleus divides first (mitosis — 4 sub-phases), then the cytoplasm splits (cytokinesis). Result: two genetically identical daughter cells. ~1 hr
The Full Cycle
G1 + S + G2 are collectively called interphase — the cell is doing preparatory work but is not yet dividing. M phase is the visible division event. Together, the cycle takes ~24 hours in a typical human cell.
Check Your Understanding
A cell spends the most time in which phase of the cell cycle, and what is it doing there?
AM phase — the cell is actively dividing and splitting its cytoplasm
BS phase — DNA replication is the most time-consuming process
CG1 phase — the cell is growing, checking nutrient levels, and preparing for DNA copying
DG2 phase — double-checking replication takes the longest
✓ Correct. G1 accounts for roughly half the total cycle time (~11 of 24 hours). During G1, the cell must satisfy multiple criteria — cell size, nutrient availability, growth factor signals — before receiving permission to copy its DNA.
Not quite. G1 is the longest phase at ~11 hours. M phase (mitosis) is actually the shortest at about 1 hour — the cell spends far more time preparing to divide than actually dividing.
Why This Matters

Understanding which phase DNA replication happens in is the foundation for everything that follows. When replication goes wrong — when errors slip through — the cell's genetic instructions are corrupted. If those errors aren't caught at a checkpoint, they get copied into every daughter cell. That is how a single mutation becomes thousands.

Section 2 of 4 · The Normal Cell Cycle
Mitosis — The M Phase in Detail
What happens when the cell finally divides
What You Will Learn in This Section
›Name and describe the four phases of mitosis: Prophase, Metaphase, Anaphase, Telophase
›Explain why the order of mitosis phases matters for maintaining genomic integrity

Mitosis is the M phase of the cell cycle — the moment when one cell physically becomes two. It happens in four ordered phases, each building on the previous one. By the end, every chromosome has been faithfully distributed: each daughter cell gets exactly one complete copy of the genome.

The Four Phases of Mitosis — PMAT
① Prophase
Chromosomes condense. During interphase, DNA exists as loose chromatin. In prophase, it coils tightly into visible, distinct chromosomes. The nuclear envelope begins to break down. The mitotic spindle — made of microtubule protein fibers — starts assembling from structures called centrosomes at each pole of the cell.
② Metaphase
Chromosomes align at the equator. Spindle fibers attach to a protein complex called the kinetochore at the centromere of each chromosome. The chromosomes are pulled to the center of the cell and line up along the metaphase plate. The Spindle Checkpoint verifies that every chromosome is properly attached before permitting the cell to proceed.
③ Anaphase
Sister chromatids separate. The cohesion proteins holding sister chromatids together are cleaved. Spindle fibers shorten, dragging one chromatid (now called a chromosome) toward each pole. At the end of anaphase, each pole has a complete, identical set of chromosomes.
④ Telophase & Cytokinesis
Two new nuclei form; cytoplasm divides. Nuclear envelopes reform around each set of chromosomes. Chromosomes de-condense back into chromatin. In cytokinesis (overlapping with telophase), a contractile ring of protein pinches the cell membrane inward until the cell splits into two genetically identical daughter cells.
Memory Aid
PMAT — Prophase, Metaphase, Anaphase, Telophase. A common mnemonic: "People Meet And Talk." The order isn't arbitrary — each phase creates the physical conditions the next phase requires.
Check Your Understanding
During which phase of mitosis does the Spindle Assembly Checkpoint operate, and what would happen if a chromosome were unattached to a spindle fiber?
AProphase — the checkpoint detects whether chromosomes have condensed properly before spindle assembly begins
BAnaphase — if a chromosome is unattached, the cell proceeds anyway and one daughter cell gets an extra chromosome
CMetaphase — if any chromosome is unattached, the MAD2 protein signals the cell to pause, preventing anaphase until all chromosomes are properly secured
DTelophase — the checkpoint verifies that each new nucleus received the correct number of chromosomes after division
✓ Correct. The Spindle Assembly Checkpoint operates during metaphase, after chromosomes have aligned at the metaphase plate but before anaphase begins. MAD2 is a sensor protein — when even one chromosome is unattached, it generates a "wait" signal that prevents the cohesion proteins from being cleaved. This ensures anaphase only occurs when every chromosome is properly secured.
Not quite. The Spindle Assembly Checkpoint operates during metaphase, when all chromosomes are supposed to be aligned and attached to spindle fibers at both kinetochores. The MAD2 protein acts as a sensor — if any chromosome is unattached, it blocks the signal that would trigger anaphase. This prevents the cell from dividing with an incorrect chromosome count.
Why This Matters

If a cell enters anaphase with even one chromosome unattached to a spindle fiber, that chromosome will be lost — one daughter cell gets an extra copy, one gets none. This condition is called aneuploidy and is found in most cancer cells. The Spindle Assembly Checkpoint is the cell's last line of defense against this exact error.

Section 3 of 4 · The Normal Cell Cycle
Checkpoints — The Cell's Quality Control
Three gates that a cell must pass through before it can divide
What You Will Learn in This Section
›Explain what a cell cycle checkpoint is and why the cell needs them
›Identify the three major checkpoints and describe what each one verifies

The cell cycle does not run automatically. At three specific points, the cell evaluates itself: Is everything ready? Is anything damaged? Should I proceed — or stop?

Analogy — The Factory Floor
Think of the cell cycle like a manufacturing process. Before a product moves to the next assembly stage, a quality inspector checks: Is this component correct? Is anything defective? Only if everything passes does the process continue. The cell uses the same logic — with proteins as inspectors instead of humans.
G1/S Checkpoint
Located at the end of G1. Verifies: Is DNA undamaged? Is the cell large enough? Are nutrients and growth factors available?

Key proteins: p53 (TP53), Rb protein.
If DNA damage is detected, p53 halts the cycle and activates DNA repair. If repair fails, p53 can trigger apoptosis — the cell destroys itself rather than pass on the damaged DNA.
G2/M Checkpoint
Located at the end of G2. Verifies: Was all DNA replicated correctly? Are there any double-strand breaks remaining?

Key proteins: ATM/ATR kinase.
ATM and ATR are sensors — they detect double-strand breaks. If breaks are present, they halt the cycle and trigger repair pathways. Only fully intact, completely replicated DNA earns permission to enter mitosis.
Spindle Assembly Checkpoint (M Phase)
Located during metaphase, inside the M phase. Verifies: Are all chromosomes properly attached to spindle fibers at both kinetochores?

Key protein: MAD2. When even one chromosome is unattached, MAD2 generates a "wait" signal that prevents anaphase. Only when every chromosome is secured does the cell proceed with division. This checkpoint prevents the creation of daughter cells with missing or extra chromosomes.
Check Your Understanding
A mutation inactivates the ATM kinase protein in a cell. Based on the checkpoint system, what is the most likely consequence?
AThe cell will divide faster than normal because ATM normally slows down DNA replication
BThe G2/M checkpoint will fail — cells with DNA double-strand breaks could enter mitosis and divide with damaged DNA
CThe G1/S checkpoint will fail — p53 depends on ATM to function at the G1 stage
DThe cell will immediately undergo apoptosis because ATM is required for all normal cell functions
✓ Correct. ATM kinase is the primary sensor at the G2/M checkpoint for double-strand DNA breaks. Without ATM, a cell that has replicated its DNA incorrectly — with breaks remaining — cannot be detected and stopped at G2. That cell proceeds into mitosis, divides, and passes the broken DNA to its daughter cells. This is a key step in the development of many cancers.
Not quite. ATM kinase is a sensor protein at the G2/M checkpoint — it detects double-strand DNA breaks after replication. Without ATM, the G2/M checkpoint cannot detect these breaks, and cells with damaged DNA can enter mitosis. The G1/S checkpoint uses a different protein (p53, activated via a different pathway) and is not directly dependent on ATM.
Why This Matters

These three checkpoints are not redundant — each catches a different category of error at a different moment. Remove any one of them and you open a specific vulnerability. In Lesson 2, you will see exactly what happens when the p53 checkpoint fails — and meet a patient whose diagnosis gives you the chance to figure out which checkpoint broke down, and why.

Section 4 of 4 · The Normal Cell Cycle
The Protein Cast
The key proteins that run the checkpoints — and what happens when they break
What You Will Learn in This Section
›Name the four key checkpoint proteins and which checkpoint each one serves
›Understand that these proteins are encoded by genes — and genes can mutate

Checkpoints don't run themselves — they are run by proteins. Proteins are produced from instructions encoded in DNA. If the DNA instructions (the gene) become mutated, the protein may stop working. In Lesson 2, you will learn what happens when these specific proteins fail.

p53
Gene: TP53
The G1/S gatekeeper. Detects DNA damage and halts the cycle. If damage is irreparable, p53 triggers apoptosis. Mutated in over 50% of all human cancers — the most commonly mutated gene in cancer.
G1/S Checkpoint
Rb Protein
Gene: RB1
A second G1/S brake. Blocks transcription factors that would push the cell into S phase. Both copies of RB1 must be inactivated to lose function — the first example of the two-hit hypothesis.
G1/S Checkpoint
ATM/ATR Kinase
Gene: ATM, ATR
Sensors at the G2/M checkpoint. Detect double-strand DNA breaks after replication is complete. Activate DNA repair pathways and, if damage is irreparable, trigger apoptosis.
G2/M Checkpoint
MAD2
Gene: MAD2L1
The spindle sentinel. Ensures every chromosome is correctly attached to spindle fibers before anaphase begins. When even one chromosome is unattached, MAD2 generates a "wait" signal.
Spindle Checkpoint
Key Concept — Genes → Proteins → Checkpoints
Every checkpoint protein is encoded by a gene. A mutation in that gene can alter or destroy the protein's function. When the protein can't do its job, the checkpoint fails. When a checkpoint fails, errors that would normally be caught can accumulate. This chain — gene mutation → protein failure → checkpoint bypass → unchecked division — is the molecular basis of cancer.
Check Your Understanding
Which of the following best explains why p53 is so important in cancer prevention?
Ap53 speeds up DNA replication in S phase, so it produces more accurate copies of the genome
Bp53 is the only protein that can detect DNA damage — without it, no checkpoint functions at all
Cp53 sits at the G1/S checkpoint — if it fails, damaged DNA is copied in S phase and the errors are inherited by every future daughter cell
Dp53 is found in every cell type, but it is only relevant in cells that divide very quickly, like skin cells
✓ Correct. p53 operates at the G1/S checkpoint — the gate before DNA replication. If p53 fails, a cell with damaged DNA enters S phase and copies that damage. Every daughter cell of that division inherits the error. Because S phase is where DNA is copied, the G1/S checkpoint is the last chance to stop an error before it becomes permanent and heritable. This is why TP53 mutations appear in such a high proportion of cancers.
Not quite. p53's importance comes from its position at the G1/S checkpoint — just before DNA replication begins. If p53 is nonfunctional, a cell with DNA damage can enter S phase and replicate that damage into every daughter cell. The G2/M checkpoint (ATM/ATR) and the Spindle Checkpoint (MAD2) are separate — they protect against different types of errors at different moments in the cycle.

Question 1 of 3 · Multiple Choice
A cell reaches the end of G2 with several unrepaired double-strand DNA breaks. Under normal conditions, what is the most likely outcome?
AThe cell proceeds directly into mitosis because the breaks will be repaired during M phase
BATM/ATR kinase detects the breaks, the G2/M checkpoint halts the cell cycle, and DNA repair mechanisms are activated
CThe G1/S checkpoint activates and sends the cell back to G1 to redo DNA replication
DThe cell immediately undergoes mitosis but the Spindle Checkpoint will catch the broken chromosomes later
✓ Correct. ATM/ATR kinase operates at the G2/M checkpoint specifically to detect double-strand breaks after replication.
The G2/M checkpoint — not the G1/S checkpoint — is the one that monitors DNA integrity after replication. ATM/ATR kinase detects double-strand breaks at this stage.
Question 2 of 3 · Multiple Choice
In which phase of the cell cycle does DNA replication occur, and what is produced?
AG1 — the cell copies its DNA in preparation for the S phase
BS phase — each chromosome is copied exactly once, producing two identical sister chromatids joined at the centromere
CG2 — the cell copies its DNA after checking that conditions are favorable
DProphase — chromosomes are replicated as they condense during the start of mitosis
✓ Correct. S phase (Synthesis phase) is dedicated entirely to DNA replication.
DNA replication occurs in S phase — the "S" stands for synthesis. Each chromosome is replicated once, producing two sister chromatids.
