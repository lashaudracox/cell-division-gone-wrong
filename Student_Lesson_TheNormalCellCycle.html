During which mitosis step does the Spindle Assembly Checkpoint operate — and what happens if a chromosome is unattached?
AProphase — detects whether chromosomes have condensed correctly

CAnaphase — the cell proceeds anyway and one daughter gets an extra chromosome

Checkpoints — The Cell's Quality Control
Three gates the cell must pass before it can divide
Checkpoint Animation
How Checkpoints Work

The cell cycle doesn't run automatically. At three points, the cell evaluates itself — checking whether it's ready and whether anything is damaged.

The Factory Analogy
Think of checkpoints as quality-control stations on an assembly line. Before a product moves to the next stage, an inspector checks: Is this correct? Is anything broken? Only if it passes does the process continue.

Check Your Understanding
A mutation inactivates ATM kinase. What is the most likely consequence for the cell?
ACells divide faster because ATM normally slows down DNA replication
BThe G2/M checkpoint fails — cells with unrepaired DNA breaks enter mitosis and pass errors to daughter cells
CThe G1/S checkpoint fails — p53 depends on ATM to function at G1
DThe cell immediately undergoes apoptosis
✓ Correct. ATM kinase is the G2/M checkpoint sensor. Without it, cells with double-strand DNA breaks can enter mitosis and divide — passing broken DNA to every daughter cell.
ATM kinase operates at the G2/M checkpoint, detecting double-strand breaks after replication. Without ATM, the G2/M gate stays open for damaged cells. The G1/S checkpoint uses a separate pathway through p53.
Section 4 of 4 · The Normal Cell Cycle
The Protein Cast
Four proteins that run the checkpoints — and what breaks when they mutate
Key Checkpoint Proteins

Each checkpoint is run by a protein. Proteins are encoded by genes. If the gene mutates, the protein stops working. If the protein stops working, the checkpoint fails. This chain is the molecular basis of cancer.

p53
Gene: TP53
Detects DNA damage at G1/S. Halts the cycle and activates repair. If repair fails, triggers apoptosis. Mutated in >50% of all cancers.
G1/S Checkpoint
Rb Protein
Gene: RB1
A second G1/S brake. Blocks transcription factors needed to enter S phase. Both copies of RB1 must mutate to fail — the classic two-hit example.
G1/S Checkpoint
ATM/ATR Kinase
Gene: ATM, ATR
Sensors at G2/M. Detect double-strand DNA breaks after replication. Activate repair pathways; trigger apoptosis if damage is irreparable.
G2/M Checkpoint
MAD2
Gene: MAD2L1
Spindle Assembly Checkpoint sensor. When any chromosome is unattached, MAD2 generates a "wait" signal that blocks anaphase from beginning.
Spindle Checkpoint
The Chain That Leads to Cancer
Gene mutation → Protein fails → Checkpoint bypassed → Damaged DNA copied → Uncontrolled division. This is the molecular basis of cancer. In Lesson 2, you will see exactly what happens when p53 fails — and meet Maya, whose case you will diagnose.
Check Your Understanding
Why is p53 sometimes called the "guardian of the genome"?
Ap53 speeds up DNA replication in S phase, producing more accurate copies
Bp53 is the only checkpoint protein — without it, no checkpoints function at all
Cp53 guards the G1/S gate — if it fails, damaged DNA gets copied in S phase and every daughter cell inherits the error
Dp53 repairs DNA directly, so without it replication errors accumulate
✓ Correct. p53 stands at the G1/S checkpoint — the last gate before DNA replication. If p53 fails, a cell with damaged DNA enters S phase and copies that damage into every future daughter cell. That's why TP53 mutations appear in over 50% of cancers.
p53's importance comes from its position at G1/S — just before DNA replication. If it fails, damaged DNA gets copied. ATM/ATR (G2/M) and MAD2 (Spindle) are separate proteins protecting against different errors at different stages.
