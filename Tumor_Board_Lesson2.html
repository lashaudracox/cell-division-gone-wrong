GROUP ACTIVITY — LESSON 2

Tumor Board: The Maya Case

A real hospital's Tumor Board is a meeting where specialists from different fields review a patient's case together and reach a consensus on diagnosis and treatment. Today your group is Maya's Tumor Board.

🏥
Real-World Connection
At children's cancer research hospitals, every complex case is reviewed by a Tumor Board — a team that includes oncologists, pathologists, genetic counselors, researchers, and patient advocates. No single specialist makes the call alone. Your job today mirrors that process exactly.
Maya's Case — Evidence Summary

Review Before You Begin

Clue 1 — Lab Report
Bone Marrow Biopsy Result
Lymphoblast count: 72% of bone marrow cells. Normal is under 5%. Rapid proliferation with no signs of apoptosis signaling.
Clue 2 — Biopsy Analysis
Cell Cycle Observation
Cells are dividing without completing G1 requirements. Cyclin D1 is overexpressed. G1/S checkpoint appears to be bypassed.
Clue 3 — Genetic Panel
Molecular Finding
Both copies of a tumor suppressor gene are inactivated. Consistent with a two-hit event. Loss of checkpoint arrest and apoptosis signaling confirmed.
Clue 4 — Guiding Question
Your Board's Task
Which checkpoint failed? What gene(s) are involved? What type of mutation — oncogene, tumor suppressor, or both? What treatment approach does the evidence support?
Your Role

Four Specialists, One Patient

Each person on the board has a specific lens. Read your role, answer your questions individually, then share with the group.

🔬
Molecular Biologist
Gene & Mutation Analysis

You interpret the genetic data. Your job is to identify exactly which gene failed and explain the molecular mechanism.

Which gene or genes does Clue 3 point to? What type of mutation is this — gain-of-function or loss-of-function? Name the specific checkpoint it affects.
Sentence starter: "The genetic panel points to _____ because both copies are inactivated, which means the cell lost its _____ function at the _____ checkpoint."
🧫
Pathologist
Tissue & Lab Interpretation

You interpret what the physical samples reveal. Your job is to connect the lab numbers and biopsy findings to what's happening at the cellular level.

What does a lymphoblast count of 72% tell you? Using Clues 1 and 2, explain what the G1/S checkpoint should have done — and clearly didn't.
Sentence starter: "A lymphoblast count of 72% indicates _____ because under normal conditions, the G1/S checkpoint would have _____."
💊
Oncologist
Treatment Strategy

You design the treatment plan. Your job is to use the molecular findings to recommend a targeted approach — not just general chemotherapy.

Based on the mutation identified, what type of treatment would specifically target it? What's the biological mechanism your treatment would need to interrupt in Maya's cell cycle?
Sentence starter: "Because Maya's cells show _____, a targeted therapy would need to _____ in order to restore _____ in the cell cycle."
🤝
Patient Advocate
Patient & Family Perspective

You speak for Maya and her family. Your job is to ask the questions a 12-year-old and her parents would need answered — and ensure the board considers the full human impact.

What are the three most important questions Maya's family would ask the Tumor Board? How would you explain — in plain language — what went wrong in her cells?
Sentence starter: "Maya's family would want to know _____, _____, and _____. In everyday terms, what happened is that her cells _____."
🗳

Board Consensus — After Group Discussion

1. Which checkpoint failed in Maya's case?
2. Mutation type in Maya's case
3. Does Maya's case fit the Two-Hit Hypothesis?
4. Group Treatment Recommendation
5. One thing your group disagrees about (or found difficult to agree on)
Whole-Class Debrief

Discussion Questions

1
Did every group reach the same consensus on which checkpoint failed? If groups disagreed, what evidence led to different conclusions? ANALYZE
2
The Oncologist had to propose a treatment without knowing all the details yet. What would they need to learn in Lesson 4 to make a more specific recommendation? EVALUATE
3
The Patient Advocate's role doesn't require knowing gene names — but the board can't work without them. What does that tell you about how science is actually communicated in medicine? EVALUATE
4
If Maya's mother also had one inactivated copy of the same tumor suppressor gene from birth, how would that change the Molecular Biologist's analysis? What term from your module describes this scenario? APPLY
