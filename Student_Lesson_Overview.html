Learning Objective — What you will be able to do by the end of this lesson
I will be able to analyze how mutations in cell cycle checkpoint genes lead to uncontrolled cell division and explain why this is the biological basis of cancer.
Why this matters
In the last lesson, you learned how a healthy cell divides. But cell division goes wrong millions of times per year in human bodies — and when the systems that control it fail, the result is cancer.

Meet Maya. Maya is 12 years old. She was recently diagnosed with Acute Lymphoblastic Leukemia (ALL) — a cancer of the white blood cells. Her genetic panel shows a mutation in a tumor suppressor gene. To understand what happened in her cells, you need to understand what checkpoint proteins do — and what happens when they don't.
🔎 Keep this question in mind: Which checkpoint failed in Maya's cells — and what does the evidence tell you?

Key vocabulary — preview before you begin
Cell Cycle Checkpoint
A pause point where the cell verifies conditions before advancing
Tumor Suppressor Gene
A gene coding for "brakes" — slows or stops division
Oncogene
A mutated gene that sends constant "divide now" signals
p53 / TP53
The G1 checkpoint protein — the "guardian of the genome"
Two-Hit Hypothesis
Both copies of a tumor suppressor must mutate to lose function
Apoptosis
Programmed cell death — a safety valve that cancer bypasses

You'll receive a new patient profile — not Maya — with a specific mutation. You'll write a structured brief: what went wrong at the cellular level, what treatment approach could target it, and what a researcher would want to know next.
