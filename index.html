Module Overview

An NGSS standards-aligned module on the
biology of cancer

Students learn how mutations in cell cycle checkpoint genes lead to uncontrolled cell division and why that's the biological basis of cancer through a flipped classroom sequence grounded in biomedical research and anchored to real patient cases.

Research Connection

Every connection is grounded in real science

Every biomedical reference in this module is tied to actual research, real patient biology, and documented clinical outcomes, not generic examples chosen for convenience.

Research Anchor

TP53 Mutations

Students analyze TP53 mutations — a tumor suppressor gene that is an active focus of pediatric cancer research at leading children's cancer research hospitals. This is documented science, not a textbook approximation.

Real Data

Genomic Data Portal

Lesson 4 introduces students to publicly accessible genomic data portals — repositories built from real pediatric cancer patients. Students engage with actual research infrastructure, not simulated data.

Patient Narrative

Meet Maya

The case study is built on the real biology of Acute lymphoblastic leukemia (ALL) — lymphoblasts, bone marrow, tumor suppressor mutations in lymphocyte development. Fictional patient, fully accurate science grounded in published clinical profiles.

Treatment Bridge

CAR-T Immunotherapy

Lesson 4 introduces CAR-T cell therapy — a direct application of the cellular biology students spent three lessons building. The treatment mechanism connects directly to the mutation biology students analyzed.

Decades of pediatric cancer research have brought the ALL survival rate from under 10% in the 1960s to over 90% today. That number is how this module closes.
Instructional Design

Every decision has a reason

Key design choices and the research that supports them.

01

Maya is the instructional anchor, not decoration

Merrill's First Principles argue that component skills are acquired most durably when taught in the context of a real problem. Every document connects back to Maya, so the summative assessment feels like transfer, not a new task.

Merrill, M.D. (2002). First principles of instruction.
02

Analogies are structural scaffolding, not style

Sweller's cognitive load theory establishes that learners lacking prior schemas must work harder to process new content. The car brake/accelerator analogy gives 9th graders a mental structure before they encounter the biology.

Sweller, J. (2019). Cognitive load theory and educational technology.
03

The teacher guide treats exit data as instructional input

Harris & Jones define formative assessment as measurement that occurs while there is still time to adjust instruction. The triage table makes that adjustment actionable. The teacher knows what to do before students walk in.

Harris, L. & Jones, M.G. (2021). Measuring student learning.
04

The simulator is application, not presentation

A video of the cell cycle is "Show." The simulator is "Do." Merrill is explicit: remembering information is insufficient. Students control the scenario, step through consequences, and encounter real-time outcomes.

Merrill, M.D. (2002). First principles of instruction.
05

ARCS model runs through the entire digital lesson

Attention (pediatric cancer statistic), Relevance (junior researcher framing), Confidence (immediate quiz feedback with explanations), Satisfaction (exit ticket closes the loop to next-day class).

Keller, J.M. (1987). Development and use of the ARCS model.
06

Guided notes scaffold without removing demand

Fill-in frames offload organizational cognitive work without removing conceptual demand. Students must retrieve and apply vocabulary, not copy from a completed answer. Two support levels: vocabulary scaffolds and open analysis rows.

Belland, Kim & Hannafin (2013). Scaffolds for PBL environments.
