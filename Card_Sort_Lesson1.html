What happens here?
Chromosomes condense and become visible under a microscope. The nuclear envelope breaks down. The mitotic spindle — made of microtubule fibers — begins to form from the centrosomes.
Drop phase here
What happens here?
All chromosomes line up precisely along the cell's equator (the metaphase plate). Spindle fibers from opposite poles attach to each chromosome's centromere. This alignment is checked before the cell can proceed.
Drop phase here
What happens here?
Sister chromatids are pulled apart. Spindle fibers shorten and drag one copy of each chromosome to opposite poles. By the end, each pole has a complete set of chromosomes.
Drop phase here
What happens here?
Nuclear envelopes reform around each set of chromosomes. Chromosomes de-condense. The cytoplasm divides (cytokinesis) — in animal cells, a contractile ring pinches the cell in two, producing two identical daughter cells.

What does this checkpoint verify?
Is the cell large enough? Is DNA undamaged? Are nutrients and growth factors available? If DNA damage is detected, p53 halts the cycle and activates repair — or triggers apoptosis if repair fails.
🔑 Key proteins: p53 (TP53), Rb protein · Location: end of G1
Drop checkpoint here
What does this checkpoint verify?
Was all DNA replicated correctly? Are there any double-strand breaks remaining? Only if DNA is complete and intact does the cell receive permission to enter mitosis.
🔑 Key proteins: ATM/ATR kinase · Location: end of G2

What does this checkpoint verify?
Are all chromosomes properly attached to spindle fibers at both kinetochores? If even one chromosome is unattached, this checkpoint prevents anaphase — ensuring each daughter cell gets exactly one copy of every chromosome.
🔑 Key protein: MAD2 · Location: during M phase (metaphase)

Group Discussion
After completing all three rounds, discuss and record your group's answers:
Q1 · Understand
The cell cycle has three major checkpoints — at G1/S, G2/M, and during M phase. Why do you think all three exist? What would happen if the cell only had the G1/S checkpoint?
Q2 · Apply
A scientist uses a drug that blocks all cells in metaphase. Based on what you learned in Round 3, which checkpoint is this drug targeting, and what protein is it likely affecting?
Q3 · Analyze — Bridge to Lesson 2
Imagine the p53 protein stopped working in a cell. Walk through the cell cycle — at which checkpoint would the first problem appear? What would the cell do that it shouldn't? Where might this lead?
