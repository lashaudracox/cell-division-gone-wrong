The Cell Cycle — Labeled Reference

Where Each Phase Fits

G1 — First Gap INTERPHASE

Cell grows, makes proteins, checks for DNA damage. Most cells spend the majority of their cycle here. Key genes: Cyclin D, CDK4/6, Rb (retinoblastoma protein).

S — Synthesis INTERPHASE

DNA is replicated — every chromosome is copied. The cell goes from 2N to 4N. Any replication errors are flagged for repair. Key genes: Cyclin E, CDK2.

G2 — Second Gap INTERPHASE

Cell checks that DNA was copied correctly and prepares the machinery for division. Damage here triggers TP53-mediated arrest. Key genes: Cyclin B, CDK1, TP53.

M — Mitosis DIVISION

Chromosomes condense, align on the spindle, and separate into two daughter cells. The spindle checkpoint verifies every chromosome is attached. Stages: Prophase → Metaphase → Anaphase → Telophase → Cytokinesis.

ONCOGENE

Stuck Accelerator

A normal proto-oncogene promotes cell growth when signaled. A mutation can turn it permanently "on" — like a gas pedal stuck to the floor. The cell divides even when it shouldn't.

Example: RAS oncogene mutation — growth signal is always active.

TUMOR SUPPRESSOR

Lost Brake

A tumor suppressor normally slows or stops the cell cycle when damage is detected. Mutation or deletion removes the "brake." Both copies must be lost for the effect to show (two-hit).

Example: TP53 mutation — apoptosis signal is silenced.

Two-Hit Hypothesis

Both Copies Must Be Lost

Most tumor suppressor genes exist in two copies (one per chromosome). Cancer requires both copies to be inactivated. This is Knudson's two-hit hypothesis.
