TP53 Mutation Rates Across Cancer Types

You identified a TP53-related checkpoint failure in Maya's case. Now go further: How does TP53 mutation frequency differ between pediatric ALL and adult cancers — and what does that tell us about how childhood cancer works?

Background

Understanding the Data

TP53 is often called the "guardian of the genome" — it's a tumor suppressor gene that triggers either DNA repair or programmed cell death (apoptosis) when a cell's DNA is damaged. It's the most commonly mutated gene across all human cancers.

But here's what's interesting: TP53 mutation rates are not the same across all cancer types. Adult cancers that develop over decades — like lung, colon, and ovarian cancer — tend to have very high TP53 mutation rates. Pediatric cancers like ALL often have relatively lower rates. This difference tells us something important about how those cancers develop.
