Objective: Students will analyze how mutations in cell cycle checkpoint genes lead to uncontrolled cell division and explain why this is the biological basis of cancer.
Instructions: Answer all three questions honestly. Your teacher will review these before class to plan tomorrow's warm-up. There are no trick questions — write what you actually think.
Question 1 of 3 · Multiple Choice
A cell's G1 checkpoint detects significant DNA damage. Under normal conditions, what is the most likely outcome?
A
The cell ignores the damage and proceeds to the S phase immediately
B
p53 is activated, the cell cycle pauses, and DNA repair mechanisms are triggered — or the cell undergoes apoptosis if the damage is irreparable
C
The cell skips G1 entirely and enters the M phase directly
D
The G2 checkpoint is notified but G1 continues normally
Question 2 of 3 · Multiple Choice
Which combination of mutations would be most likely to result in uncontrolled cell division?
A
A mutation that slightly slows DNA replication during S phase
B
A single mutated copy of a tumor suppressor gene
C
A mutation in a non-coding region with no effect on protein production
D
An activated oncogene (always-on growth signal) and loss of both copies of a tumor suppressor gene
Question 3 of 3 · Short Answer
Name one gene or protein that acts as a cell cycle checkpoint regulator. If this gene were to mutate and stop working, predict what might happen to the cell. Use evidence from what you learned in this module.
💡 Think about: Which gene? What does it normally do? What happens if both copies are mutated? Use vocabulary from the Word Bank.

Triage Table

Pattern You SeePileFirst 7 Minutes of Class
Both MC correct and Q3 names a specific gene with mechanism + consequence Proficient ✓ Assign Maya case study immediately — they're ready. While others warm up, give them the extension prompt: "What second mutation would make Scenario 3 (RAS only) become cancer?"
MC correct but Q3 only restates the question or uses vague language ("the gene that stops cancer") Partial Warm-up: "Look at your Word Bank. Point to one gene. Write one sentence: what does it do when it's working correctly?" Then cold-call before Maya case begins.
One or both MC wrong or Q3 is blank / shows fundamental confusion (e.g., "mutations are always bad") Needs Support Do NOT start Maya case yet. Run a 5-minute Fist-to-Five on the car analogy: "Hold up fingers for how confident you are about the brakes/accelerator model." Then re-teach in 2 minutes before grouping.
Q1 correct but Q2 wrong (chose B — single copy) — common misconception Partial Targeted question for the class: "How many copies of a tumor suppressor gene does a cell have? What has to happen to BOTH copies?" Draw the two-hit diagram on the board.
Q3 short answer contradicts a correct MC answer (shows guessing on MC) Needs Support Pair these students with Proficient students for the Maya case. Give them a specific job: "Your partner explains the gene mutation clue. You explain what that means for the checkpoint."
